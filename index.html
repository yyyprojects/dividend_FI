A Visual Guide to Dividends

Understanding how companies share profits with you, the investor.

What is a Dividend?

It's a share of a company's profits, paid directly to a shareholder (owner).

When a profitable company has cash left over, its board of directors faces a key choice: reinvest the money for growth or distribute it to shareholders.

That distribution is a dividend.

The Lifecycle of a Dividend Payment

From announcement to payment, every dividend follows four critical dates. Owning the stock before the 'Ex-Dividend Date' is the key to getting paid.

1

Declaration Date

The company's board officially announces the dividend, its amount, and the payment schedule.

2

Ex-Dividend Date

The cutoff day. You must buy the stock *before* this date to receive the dividend. The stock exchange sets this date.

3

Record Date

The company checks its books to see who the official shareholders are. If you're on the list, you get paid.

4

Payment Date

Payday! The cash (or stock) is actually delivered to the eligible shareholders' accounts.

The #1 Metric: Dividend Yield

Yield measures the dividend relative to the stock price, allowing for easy comparison.

Warning: An unusually high yield can be a red flag that the stock price has fallen, not a sign of a healthy dividend.

How dividends are taxed makes a huge difference. "Qualified" dividends get much better tax rates.
